why do we experience the ick?

Sunday 21st December 2025, 19:47

A little dive into what the “ick” is and why we get it.

Imagine this: you’re about to meet someone on a first date. You don’t really know them, but your friend said you’d be a good match, and they are just your type on paper; you both want a relationship, you’re in the same stage of life, they’ve had a good track record and you’ve seen a picture….hottie! You get yourself all ready in the outfit you prepared last night, a spritz of your favourite perfume, call your friends and take a thousand selfies. Ready!

Fast forward, and the date is going well, really well. They look even better in person, and they’re charming, and conversation is flowing, and they’re even funny!? You’re positively buzzing by the time the date ends, and you lean in for a goodbye hug, and they give you a….awkward side hug? Ew. You’re snapped back to reality. The world is back in its dull shades and you do NOT want to be in contact with this person any longer, get them away from me! You give a muted smile, and they offer to drive you back home, but you politely decline (even though that would’ve been incredibly convenient). You bus back home and text your friend that they’re not the one for you :(. They’re confused. You try and explain it's because of a hug, but they just don’t get it. Everything else was perfect, so why does this small little thing turn you off so much? Are you being dramatic? You’re definitely being dramatic. Right?

Well, you just experienced the “ick”. A new popular word skittering around everywhere; some people get it, and others don’t. What is the ick and why does it elicit such a strong (irrational) reaction in us? There’s 1 main (not so) scientific explanation: it's a you problem, not a them problem. We can further break this down into 2 (pseudo) scientific reasons: you don’t actually like this person; and you have inner problems that need fixing first.

Lets tackle the first reason, that you don’t actually like like this person. Dating is universally found difficult, and it can be so hard to find that *spark*, you can feel it in the air and your heart, tinkling and humming when you meet someone that you would consider your type or completely surprise you in someone you normally wouldn’t go for. Sometimes, the *spark* doesn’t show up when you expect it to. They could be exactly your type on paper, have all the qualities you’re looking for, be kind and (importantly) into you, but…you’re just not feeling it. Maybe you lie to yourself for a while, I’ll feel that spark soon, why would I want to waste an opportunity with someone so perfect? Your brain says one thing, but your heart knows something else. Then, when the smallest reason presents itself to call it off completely, you latch on. You’re searching and hungering for a “why”. A why I don’t like this person, a why I can tell them it won’t work for us, a why I can tell my friends, a why to just cover up the unfortunate truth: you didn’t really like them in the first place. Something, anything to soothe the rejection and explain yourself.

I’m sure we’ve all been in a situation where we liked someone so much that we let things slide that we really shouldn’t. You look back on it, and you cringe at how much of a limp doormat you became, why did you accept that? Why didn’t you leave? Simple, because you had feelings and you thought you could let a few things go if it meant staying with them. Here, we’re on the opposite end, the person is actually wonderful! But you wish you could find something to tell them no.

Then, let’s look at the other reason: you’re the issue. Commitment issues? Fear of intimacy? Fear of closeness? You’ve been hurt before? Never been in a relationship? Projecting insecurities? Any of these sound familiar? You have your own problems that need working through, and you’re too scared to get too close to someone again, and when your brain sees a sign that this person actually has a shot at getting close to you, that they pass every prior check and meet all your standards, you don’t know how to respond. Either this is unfamiliar territory, or painful. This could be completely new, or the last time you got badly wounded. So to protect yourself, you self-sabotage. Again, we’re searching for a why we can cut things off. A why to explain to everyone else why it just wouldn’t work out. But the real reason is your own fear. A fear of the unknown and the possibility of getting hurt again. So you come up with a nonsensical reason of why.

This reason may also reflect on your own insecurities. We’re drawn to people we want to be like, people we admire and perhaps see our own positive qualities in. We’re also repelled from those who remind us too much of our own weaknesses and issues. Does this person remind you too much of an insecurity you have? Were you always self-conscious about how loud your voice is, and right infront of you is someone else who’s also very loud? Do they stutter like you do? Bite their lip when anxious like you do? Have FOMO like you do? Hold their chopsticks funny like you do? When someone has some of these dreaded flaws we see in ourselves, it can be like a constant reminder, a mirror of what’s wrong with you. Ew. So we run away, we don’t like that they’re too loud, we don’t like that they stutter, we don’t like that they bite their lip, we don’t like how they’re scared of missing out, we don’t like that they can’t hold their chopsticks.

Maybe you can try to explain all this to your friends, but you don’t even fully get it yourself, you just know that something is telling you NO! And it’s good to listen to this voice, you don’t want to hold yourself hostage in a relationship you don’t actually want, but it’s always good to dig a bit deeper into why you’re saying no. Why are you rejecting this person, really?
